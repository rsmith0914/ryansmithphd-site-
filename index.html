About me

the short version

I hold a Bachelor's in Psychology from Marist College and a Master's in Social and Cognitive Psychology from NYU, where I focused on decision making.

After my studies I worked as a research assistant in two psychology labs, the DUSC Lab and the Visual Thinking Lab, before applying to PhD programs.

I was initially interested in neuroaesthetics (the neuroscience of art), but pivoted to data visualization after encountering an art installation that used visualization to explain a complex topic. I started wondering if people actually study this kind of work (they do) and began working in the visualization field.

I'm now pursuing my PhD in Computer Science at UBC, working with Tamara Munzner in the InfoVis Lab.

Research interests

what I actually work on

Patients don't narrate their medical history like a form. They jump around in time, organize around their own concerns, hedge their language, and rely on context and lived experience. These narratives are rich, but existing healthcare tools strip all of that away, forcing everything into discrete, standardized fields.

My research sits at that gap. I build systems that meet patients in their own words, whether they're speaking, writing, or describing their experiences however feels natural, and transform that freeform input into structured visualizations that can travel into a clinical encounter and actually be useful.

I work in collaboration with Thrive Health, connecting the research to real clinical workflows, and under Tamara Munzner in UBC's InfoVis Lab.

Current projects

things I'm building

HealthTale — visualizing your health story

When you describe your health history to a new doctor, it probably doesn't flow neatly. You jump to what feels most important, organize around concerns rather than dates, use language like "a few years back" or "right around when I started that job." Health histories are organized around meaning, not chronology.

HealthTale meets patients in that natural way of speaking. You describe your health history however feels right, and the system transforms it into a structured, timeline visualization you can bring into a clinical encounter. The goal is to help patients feel heard and help clinicians understand not just the facts, but how the patient frames their own experience.

SymptomTale — tracking what's hard to track

Most symptom tracking tools ask you to rate your pain from 1 to 10. But what if you don't know what to call what you're feeling? What if it shifts, or it's not one symptom but a cluster of things that seem connected but you're not sure how?

SymptomTale lets you describe your symptoms in plain language, spoken or written, whenever it makes sense to you. The system builds a longitudinal visualization that's comfortable with uncertainty: timing can be approximate, severity can be qualitative, and the picture can evolve as your understanding does. The goal is something you'd actually want to use, even when what you're tracking is hard to name.

Teaching

how I show up in the classroom

Being a student is stressful. Heavy course loads, constant evaluation, and pressure to perform. In that environment, memorization has limited long-term value. What matters more is whether students leave curious and motivated to keep learning. I see my role not just as delivering content, but as helping create that outcome, where students develop the interest and confidence to continue learning beyond the course.

This perspective shapes how I design and support teaching. I emphasize flexible engagement through optional materials and use class time for small-group activities focused on applying ideas and working through ambiguity. I approach teaching transparently, treating students as adults with different goals, and aim to create an environment where participation feels natural. My evaluation follows a pass / fail / exceed structure, reducing pressure while encouraging deeper engagement, with the overall goal of fostering a low-stakes but meaningful learning environment.

Service & leadership

showing up for the grad community

Alongside research and teaching, I've spent much of my PhD working to improve the graduate student experience, primarily by advocating for better mental-health access and breaking down silos between departments.

I've served as Vice President and now President of the Computer Science Graduate Student Association, a councillor on the UBC Graduate Student Society's Executive Oversight Committee, and a CUPE 2278 union steward. Out of that work came two initiatives I care about most: pushing for an embedded counselor within the Faculty of Science so grad students could have the same access as other faculties, and running inter-departmental socials across Math, Psychology, Music, and adjacent programs so students could find collaborators and friends outside their home lab.

This work was recognized with the UBC Faculty of Science Excellence in Service Award, given annually to two students in the Faculty. It's probably the part of my PhD I'm most proud of!
